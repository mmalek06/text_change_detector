# ADR 001: Prominence-based boundary detection instead of an absolute floor plus a robust z-score

- **Status:** Accepted, implemented
- **Date:** 2026-07-08
- **Scope:** the tiling stage (`src/text_change_detector/tiling/pipeline.py`), specifically how a gap between two sentences becomes a semantic-unit boundary.

## Context

The tiler splits a document into semantic units by deciding, at each gap between two consecutive sentences, whether a topic boundary sits there.

### The signal (unchanged by this decision)

The signal it decides on is a **windowed dissimilarity** produced by `_step_dissimilarities`. For each gap it takes up to four sentences on the left and up to four on the right, joins each side into one piece of text, embeds both, and computes `d = 1 - cosine(left, right)`. A high `d` means the two sides read as different topics.

The four-sentence window matters and is worth stating plainly, because it carries a property people often attribute to the threshold instead: a boundary registers only when a **sustained** change accumulates. A single off-topic sentence is diluted one-in-four inside the window, so it barely moves `d`. As the right window fills with sentences of a new topic, `d` rises to a peak and then falls. This "several sentences, not one" behaviour lives entirely in the window and is independent of how we later pick boundaries from `d`.

### The old decision rule (what this ADR replaces)

In `_build_groups`, a gap `k` became a boundary if **either** of two tests fired:

1. **Absolute floor:** `d[k] >= 1 - floor`, with `floor = 0.6`. In plain terms: cut when the window cosine similarity drops to 0.6 or below.
2. **Robust z-score (`_is_boundary`):** cut when `d[k]` stands out from its local neighbourhood, measured as `(d[k] - local_median) / local_MAD > 3.0` over a window of 31 gaps. `MAD` is the median absolute deviation, a robust (outlier-resistant) stand-in for the standard deviation.

The intent was that the z-score would be a relative, self-adapting test and the floor a safety net.

## What was wrong with it

Experiments 03 to 06 (see `experiments/`) measured this directly and found three problems.

1. **The z-score was redundant.** On a 50-page statute it fired on 16 of 292 boundaries, and every one of those 16 was already cut by the floor. Turning it off changed nothing: the floor did essentially 100% of the cutting.

2. **The z-score was also broken where it was supposed to help.** Two independent reasons. First, `MAD` has a 50% breakdown point: when boundaries are dense (about 29% of gaps in the statute), the local neighbourhood is itself full of high values, so the local median and MAD rise to meet them and nothing stands out any more. Second, the threshold `3.0` is calibrated for rare anomalies; even in clean, uncrowded neighbourhoods a real boundary only reached about 1.2 robust standard deviations, nowhere near 3. So the "adaptive" test under-fired badly on exactly the documents it was meant for.

3. **The floor was an embedder-coupled magic constant.** `0.6` is a raw cosine value, and cosine scales differ wildly between embedding models. The identical `0.6`, over the same text, cut a different number of gaps on every embedder we tried: 11 and 29 on two English models, 232 and **0** on two others. Zero means the pipeline produced no semantic segmentation at all (on a model whose cosines sit very high, `d` never reached 0.4). A single hardcoded number cannot span "cut nothing" to "cut everything", so the tiler could silently break on an embedder swap, and the README's claim that the test was relative and uncalibrated did not hold: the operative rule was the absolute constant.

## What prominence is

Think of the dissimilarity `d` as a one-dimensional curve plotted over the gaps. A topic boundary appears as a **peak**: `d` rises as the window crosses into a new topic and falls once it is past.

A *peak* is any point higher than its immediate neighbours. But most little bumps are noise, so we need a way to say how much a peak actually stands out. That measure is **prominence**.

Prominence of a peak is defined like a hill on a map: starting at the peak, walk down along the curve in each direction until you either reach the end or start climbing toward some higher peak. On each side note the lowest point you passed (the valley, or saddle). The prominence is the height of the peak **above the higher of those two valleys**.

```
   d
   |            peak
   |             /\
   |   left     /  \
   |  valley __/    \
   |         /       \___ right valley (lower)
   |________/             \________
   +-------------------------------> gap index

   prominence = peak height - height of the HIGHER valley (here the left one)
```

Intuitively, prominence is how far you must descend from a peak before you could reach any higher ground. A small bump sitting on a high plateau has low prominence (you barely descend). A tall peak rising out of deep valleys on both sides has high prominence.

Two properties make it the right tool here:

- **It is a height difference** (peak minus valley), so shifting or scaling the whole curve leaves prominences essentially unchanged. Swapping embedders shifts and scales the cosine range, so this is exactly the invariance the absolute floor lacked.
- **It is local**, reading only the nearest valleys, not a wide statistical window. So it does not get poisoned when boundaries are dense, which is what broke the z-score.

The new decision rule: a gap is a boundary if it is a peak whose prominence clears a cutoff derived from the signal's own prominences, `mean - c * std`, with `c = 1`. In practice the cutoff is flat: for any `c >= 1` it reduces to "cut at every prominence peak", so `c` is a gentle granularity dial on a scale-relative distribution, not a cliff like `0.6`.

Prominence also matches the "sustained change" intuition more faithfully than the z-score did. It measures the actual size of the rise-and-fall, so a single diluted bump has small prominence and is not cut, whereas the z-score, in a locally calm stretch, could flag that same small bump because the local MAD was tiny.

## Decision

Replace the floor plus z-score with prominence-based boundary detection inside `_build_groups`. Everything else stays: the windowed signal (`_step_dissimilarities`), the bidirectional growing window, `force_grow`, `group_max_len`, deduplication, the kNN graph and Louvain.

## Evidence

- **Quality (production embedder):** prominence recovers more true boundaries with fewer cuts. On a hand-labelled synthetic passage, 92% recall versus 67% for floor plus z-score; on the statute's article boundaries, 77% versus 50%.
- **Robustness (across embedders):** prominence's cut count and recall stay in the same range on every model, while the floor's cut count swings from 232 to 10 and its recall drops to 0% on one model.
- **One flat knob:** any `c >= 1` gives the same result as "all peaks", so there is nothing brittle to tune.

## Consequences

**Positive**

- Embedder-independent behaviour; the tiler no longer silently collapses on a model swap.
- One flat, scale-relative knob (`prominence_c`) replaces three brittle ones.
- Higher boundary recall and fewer over-segmented units.
- Slightly faster (`find_peaks` is linear; the old per-gap MAD window was not).
- The "sustained change" property is preserved, because it always came from the window, not the threshold.

**Breaking**

- `tile()` loses `floor`, `baseline_radius`, and `threshold`, and gains `prominence_c` (default 1.0). Callers passing the old arguments will error.
- Segmentation changes for every document (unit ids, unit counts, communities), so previously produced tilings are not reproducible under the new rule, and `detect_changes` will return different impacts even though its own code is unchanged.
- `_is_boundary` and the `scipy.stats.median_abs_deviation` import are removed; `scipy.signal.find_peaks` is added.
- Tests in `tests/tiling/test_pipeline.py` that exercise `_is_boundary` or pass `floor` / `threshold` to `_build_groups` must be rewritten, and the README knob list and mechanism description must be updated.

**Edge cases introduced by `find_peaks`**

- The first and last gap can never be peaks (a peak needs a neighbour on both sides), so a boundary at the very start or end of a document is not detected as a peak. The growing window closes document ends anyway, so this is marginal.
- A very short or perfectly flat document yields no peaks and therefore no cuts; the document then falls back to fixed-size units bounded by `group_max_len`. This is acceptable but should be a conscious expectation.

**Unrelated risk surfaced during the work**

- `group_max_len` makes a dead boundary detector look healthy: with almost no cuts it still emits about `n / 7` units by chopping at the cap, so a broken detector fails silently rather than obviously. This is not caused or fixed by this decision. A guard or warning when the semantic cut count is far below the unit count would make such a failure loud.

## Alternatives considered

- **Keep the floor but make it embedder-relative** (a percentile of the observed dissimilarity, or a global robust z-score). Rejected: a global threshold is arithmetically just an adaptive floor (in the experiments the top-k gaps by global z-score were exactly the floor's cuts). It stays one global cut with no locality and would misfire on documents whose regions have different variance (some smooth, some turbulent).
- **Lower the z-score threshold.** Rejected: that only relocates the magic constant, and a low threshold would fire on single-sentence bumps in calm regions.
- **Use document structure (for example legal article markers) as boundary priors.** Rejected as out of scope: this library stays embedding-based. A caller who needs structural boundaries passes already-cut segments in, or customizes the detection prompts.

## Caveats

While this library tries to be a generic one, it's obvious that there are many more document structures and topics I could test it on. If it doesn't work at all - 
it doesn't. That's a risk me as an author agrees with: it's generic until it hits an edge case. Maybe I'll fix it, maybe I won't.
